# Equity Plus Token Warrant

Use this file for hybrid raises where investors buy company exposure now and conditional token exposure later.

## When this structure fits

Choose `Equity + Token Warrant` when:
- the product likely requires a token later
- the token is not ready for direct sale or should not be the primary fundraising object yet
- investors want equity downside protection plus upside to the future network
- the story must show both company execution and token rationale without forcing premature tokenization

This is often the best bridge for serious web3 or network projects that still need product, legal, and operating flexibility.

## Core narrative

The deck must answer two questions cleanly:
- how the company creates value
- why a future token is necessary to the product or network

The investor should not leave confused about whether value accrues to the company, the token, or both.

## Recommended size

Use roughly `12-16 slides`.

Keep the token part compact: usually `2-4 token-focused slides`, not a second deck glued onto the first.

## Recommended slide order

1. `Title + one-liner`
2. `Problem`
3. `Solution / product wedge`
4. `Why now`
5. `Market + ICP`
6. `Competition / differentiation`
7. `Business model for the company`
8. `Go-to-market to product adoption`
9. `Token rationale`
10. `Token design principles and rights`
11. `Value capture split / flywheel`
12. `Team`
13. `Roadmap and launch readiness gates`
14. `Ask: equity terms plus token rights`
15. `Risks and mitigations`

If helpful, add appendix slides for legal structure, cap table, and token terms.

## What must be clear

### Why the token exists

Explain why the product needs a token at all. Good reasons usually include:
- network security
- access or usage mechanics
- coordination or governance
- incentive design

If the token is not necessary, the hybrid structure weakens.

### Token design at this stage

Show principles, not fake certainty. Early-stage hybrid decks usually need:
- token purpose
- high-level allocation logic
- insider versus community philosophy
- vesting and lockup principles
- restrictions on transfer or execution if relevant

Avoid presenting unfinished tokenomics as if they are final.

### Rights and restrictions

Explain the warrant or side-letter logic in a way investors can actually follow:
- what the investor gets
- how rights relate to equity ownership
- whether rights are dilutable
- lockups and vesting
- what happens before and after token generation
- what happens if the company exits before token launch

Prefer alignment over predatory fixed claims on the future network.

### Value capture logic

One of the biggest hybrid risks is value-capture confusion. Show how:
- company revenue and equity value grow
- token utility and ecosystem value grow
- the two systems reinforce rather than cannibalize each other

A flywheel or relationship diagram can help if the mechanics are real and simple.

## Good supporting visuals

Useful hybrid visuals include:
- legal-entity map if the structure matters
- company-to-network flywheel
- token-rights summary
- pro forma cap table plus token allocation principles
- launch-readiness gates before TGE

## Major risks to address

- token design risk
- regulatory and disclosure risk
- operational launch risk
- misalignment between equity holders and token holders
- double-dipping or unclear economics
- rushing tokenization before product need is established

## Tone and language

Do not market the hybrid structure as a shortcut to fast token liquidity. Explain possible liquidity paths carefully and only when credible. The stronger hybrid story is disciplined design plus controlled optionality.

## DD support to prepare

Useful hybrid follow-up materials:
- equity terms summary
- token warrant or side-letter summary
- pro forma cap table
- draft token policy or token memo
- lockup and vesting principles
- launch-readiness checklist
- legal structure diagram if relevant
- security, custody, and distribution readiness notes
