# Shared Principles

Use this file for all three raise types before loading a raise-specific reference.

## 1. What a pre-market deck is doing

At `pre-product` or `pre-market` stage, investors are not buying historical performance. They are evaluating:
- clarity of the problem
- credibility of the proposed solution
- size and focus of the opportunity
- right-to-win of the team
- whether this round removes the next layer of risk

The deck should therefore package hypotheses, proof signals, and a de-risking plan.

## 2. Core investor questions

A strong deck helps an investor answer:
- Why does this matter now?
- Why is this team suited to win?
- What evidence exists even before scaled traction?
- What exactly is being sold in the round?
- What does the raise unlock before the next financing step?

## 3. Universal slide logic

Most strong pre-market decks, regardless of raise type, revolve around:
- title and one-liner
- problem
- solution or product wedge
- why now
- market and ICP
- competition or current alternatives
- route to adoption
- team
- ask, use of funds, runway, milestones

What changes by raise type is the center of gravity:
- `Equity`: company, customer, business model, GTM
- `Equity + Token Warrant`: company plus future token logic
- `Token-only`: network, utility, token design, compliance posture

## 4. Proof ladder for early-stage teams

Replace missing revenue with the strongest available proof:
- problem proof: interviews, quantified pain, workflow inefficiency, urgency
- demand proof: LOIs, design partners, pilots, waitlist quality
- execution proof: prototype, architecture, shipped demo, audits, testnet
- team proof: founder-market fit, prior execution, domain edge

For token-related raises, add:
- utility proof
- community quality over vanity follower counts
- testnet or ecosystem activity
- security and launch-readiness evidence

## 5. Deck package, not just slides

Separate the fundraising materials by job:

### Presentation deck

Use for live pitching.
- lighter text
- stronger visuals
- cleaner narrative flow
- fewer appendix details

### Investor deck or send-ahead deck

Use for email or async review.
- more explicit explanation
- more context in slide bodies
- denser backup material
- easier to read without narration

### Appendix and DD support

Use for investor follow-ups.
- cap table or pro forma
- financial model or runway logic
- technical or product appendix
- legal structure summary
- token terms or compliance documents if relevant

## 6. Storytelling rules

- One thought per slide.
- Each slide should remove one major investor doubt.
- Use causal visuals, not decorative visuals.
- Prefer bottom-up logic over giant top-down TAM claims.
- Show how funds map to milestones and why those milestones matter.
- If the team is unusually strong, move team earlier.

## 7. Review rubric

When reviewing a deck, score it across:
- audience fit
- raise type fit
- strength of the proof ladder
- clarity of the ask
- clarity of value capture
- realism of milestones
- credibility of risk treatment
- DD readiness

## 8. Language guardrails

Especially for token scenarios:
- avoid price predictions
- avoid guaranteed-return language
- avoid promising liquidity or listing outcomes
- avoid mixing community growth claims with investor-return claims

Keep statements specific, factual, and consistent with what the project can defend.

## 9. Common cross-type mistakes

- confusing the product story with the financing story
- showing too many slides and too little logic
- asking for money before explaining why now
- presenting hype instead of evidence
- leaving the use of funds vague
- hiding the hardest risks instead of framing mitigations
- treating the deck like the full DD package
