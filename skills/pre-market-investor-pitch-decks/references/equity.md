# Equity

Use this file for classic early-stage raises in shares, SAFE, note, or priced equity rounds.

## When Equity is the right default

Choose `Equity` when:
- the token is optional, premature, or not product-critical
- the investor base is mostly traditional angels, seed funds, micro-VC, or strategic operators
- the company still needs to prove customer demand, GTM, and execution more than mechanism design
- the legal and fundraising perimeter should stay as simple as possible

For unresolved situations, `Equity` is the safest default.

## Goal and audience

The goal is usually to win the next meeting or term sheet by showing:
- strong problem selection
- credible wedge and GTM path
- capable team
- disciplined use of capital

Investors read the deck as a filter for quality of thinking, not as a finished operating report.

## Recommended size

Use roughly `10-14 slides`, with appendix for:
- financial model
- cap table or pro forma
- deeper product or market backup

## Recommended slide order

1. `Title + one-liner`
2. `Problem`
3. `Solution / product wedge`
4. `Why now`
5. `Market + ICP`
6. `Competition / current alternatives`
7. `Business model`
8. `Go-to-market`
9. `Traction substitutes / proof ladder`
10. `Team`
11. `Ask + use of funds + runway + milestones`
12. `Risks and mitigations` if useful
13. `Vision` if it sharpens ambition

If the founding team is the main differentiator, move `Team` earlier.

## What investors need to see

### Problem

Make the pain concrete and preferably measurable. Show why the problem is severe, frequent, expensive, or strategically important.

### Solution

Explain the wedge, not the full roadmap. Show what changes for the customer in the first meaningful adoption window.

### Why now

This is often one of the highest-leverage slides. Tie the opportunity to real shifts:
- technology
- regulation
- workflows
- buyer behavior
- distribution changes

### Market

Use focused, bottom-up logic where possible. Avoid "1 percent of a huge market" slides without a believable beachhead.

### Business model and GTM

Show how the company will get first customers, not just eventual scale. For pre-market teams, specificity beats polish.

## Financing section

Show the deal simply:
- instrument type
- target raise size
- key terms if known
- runway created
- milestones funded

If relevant, add a pro forma cap table in appendix or as a simple summary slide. Investors benefit from seeing dilution logic even when using SAFE structures.

## Financial expectations at pre-market stage

Do not pretend forecasts are precise. Instead show:
- assumptions sheet
- base, upside, downside scenarios
- monthly burn and runway
- hiring plan
- milestone-based budget logic

The model should be inspectable and connected to the execution plan.

## Good proof substitutes

Use the strongest available:
- customer discovery artifacts
- LOIs or pilots
- prototype or demo
- early design partners
- evidence of distribution access
- technical feasibility evidence

## Common mistakes

- too much product feature detail and too little market logic
- weak or missing why-now argument
- giant TAM slide without believable beachhead
- no GTM path before asking for capital
- no milestone framing for the raise
- treating team slide as biography instead of right-to-win
- forcing an exit slide too early unless it clearly adds strategic context

## DD support to prepare

Useful follow-up materials:
- cap table and option pool view
- corporate docs
- IP ownership chain
- budget and runway model
- customer discovery notes
- LOIs, pilots, or partnership evidence
- product roadmap and technical architecture summary
